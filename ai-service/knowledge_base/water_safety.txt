# Latenza Vita — Water Quality Knowledge Base
# Based on WHO, BIS IS-10500, CPCB, and Jal Jeevan Mission guidelines

## WHO Drinking Water Standards
- Safe pH range for drinking water: 6.5 to 8.5
- Maximum turbidity: 1 NTU (WHO), 4 NTU (EPA)
- Total Dissolved Solids (TDS): 500 mg/L preferred, 1000 mg/L maximum
- Zero coliform bacteria per 100 mL sample
- Water temperature: ideally 10-15 degrees Celsius
- Residual chlorine: 0.2 to 0.5 mg/L at point of use
- Arsenic: 0.01 mg/L maximum
- Lead: 0.01 mg/L maximum
- Nitrate: 50 mg/L maximum
- Fluoride: 1.5 mg/L maximum

## pH and Water Chemistry
- pH below 6.5 = acidic water — corrodes pipes, leaches lead and copper
- pH above 8.5 = alkaline water — bitter taste, scaling, reduced disinfection
- pH below 5.0 or above 10.0 = immediate supply shutdown required
- Causes of low pH: acid rain, industrial discharge, decaying organic matter, mining runoff
- Causes of high pH: limestone bedrock, industrial alkaline waste, algae blooms
- Treatment for low pH: lime addition, soda ash
- Treatment for high pH: CO2 injection, dilution with treated water

## Turbidity and Water Clarity
- Turbidity is measured in NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units)
- Below 1 NTU: WHO standard for drinking water
- Below 4 NTU: EPA maximum for filtered water
- 4-10 NTU: elevated — requires increased coagulation
- Above 10 NTU: high — potential pathogen harboring
- Above 100 NTU: extreme — likely flooding or major pollution event
- Causes: heavy rainfall, surface runoff, algae bloom, construction, industrial discharge
- High turbidity blocks UV disinfection and reduces chlorine effectiveness
- Treatment: coagulation, flocculation, sedimentation, sand filtration

## Bacterial Contamination
- Zero total coliform bacteria per 100 mL is the WHO absolute standard
- E. coli indicates direct fecal contamination — immediate emergency response
- Causes: sewage overflow, flooding, broken distribution pipes, animal waste runoff
- High bacteria risk conditions: post-flooding, monsoon season, pipe ruptures
- Health risks: cholera, typhoid fever, dysentery, gastroenteritis, hepatitis A
- Treatment: chlorination (0.5 mg/L minimum), UV disinfection, boiling (1 minute)
- Post-detection: boil water advisory, network flushing, lab confirmation within 2 hours

## Chemical Contamination
- Common contaminants: arsenic, lead, nitrates, pesticides, fluoride, chromium, mercury
- Industrial sources: heavy metals, solvents, petroleum, dyes, acids, alkalis
- Agricultural sources: nitrates, phosphates, pesticides, herbicides, insecticides
- Arsenic causes: skin lesions, keratosis, bladder/lung cancer with long-term exposure
- Lead causes: neurological damage in children, kidney disease, cardiovascular effects
- Nitrate causes: blue baby syndrome (methemoglobinemia) in infants under 6 months
- Chemical contamination: do NOT boil — boiling concentrates most chemical contaminants
- Treatment: reverse osmosis, activated carbon filtration, ion exchange, coagulation

## Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)
- TDS measures all dissolved minerals, salts, and metals in water
- Below 300 mg/L: excellent quality
- 300-600 mg/L: good quality
- 600-900 mg/L: fair — requires treatment for sensitive populations
- 900-1200 mg/L: poor — not recommended for regular consumption
- Above 1200 mg/L: unacceptable — do not use for drinking or cooking
- High TDS sources: industrial effluents, agricultural runoff, natural rock minerals
- Treatment: reverse osmosis, distillation, electrodialysis, nanofiltration

## Waterborne Diseases and Health Impacts
- Cholera: Vibrio cholerae — severe diarrhea, dehydration, spreads rapidly in communities
- Typhoid fever: Salmonella typhi — fever, abdominal pain, systemic infection, 10-14 day incubation
- Dysentery: Shigella bacteria or Entamoeba histolytica — bloody diarrhea, intestinal ulcers
- Hepatitis A: viral infection — jaundice, liver damage, 15-50 day incubation, spreads fecal-oral
- Cryptosporidiosis: Cryptosporidium parasite — severe diarrhea, resistant to chlorination
- Giardiasis: Giardia parasite — intestinal infection, bloating, diarrhea
- Arsenic poisoning: chronic — skin lesions, cancers, cardiovascular disease
- Lead poisoning: irreversible neurological damage in children under 6 years
- Fluorosis: dental mottling, skeletal damage from fluoride above 1.5 mg/L

## Emergency Response Procedures
- DANGER level: issue boil-water advisory within 15 minutes of detection
- Stop distribution from affected zone immediately for bacterial or chemical contamination
- Deploy mobile water tankers within 2 hours for emergency community supply
- Notify District Collector and Chief Medical Officer within 30 minutes
- Dispatch rapid-response field team for on-site sampling and verification
- Collect samples for laboratory analysis within 1 hour of alert
- Inspect upstream sources: industrial discharge points, sewage outfalls, agricultural areas
- Flush distribution network with high-dose chlorinated water after bacterial alert
- Test water every 2 hours during emergency until 3 consecutive safe readings
- Document all incidents for regulatory compliance — maintain 5-year log
- Coordinate with State Disaster Management Authority for large-scale flooding events
- Issue public communication through SMS alerts, local radio, and social media

## Municipal Water Treatment Process
- Step 1 Coagulation: add alum (aluminum sulfate) to clump suspended particles
- Step 2 Flocculation: gentle slow mixing to form larger clumps called floc
- Step 3 Sedimentation: allow floc to settle at bottom of settling tanks
- Step 4 Filtration: pass through rapid sand filters and slow sand filters
- Step 5 Disinfection: chlorine dosing (primary), UV treatment (secondary)
- Step 6 pH adjustment: add lime to raise pH or CO2 to lower pH
- Step 7 Fluoridation: add fluoride to 0.7 mg/L for dental health
- Step 8 Distribution: maintain 0.2 mg/L residual chlorine throughout network

## Rainfall and Water Quality
- Heavy rainfall above 50mm causes significant turbidity increase within 2-4 hours
- Flooding events contaminate water with sewage, chemicals, and industrial waste
- Post-rain quality typically recovers within 24-72 hours with treatment
- Agricultural areas risk pesticide and nitrate contamination after rainfall
- Urban areas risk heavy metal and hydrocarbon contamination after rainfall
- Monitor for delayed contamination 6-12 hours after heavy rainfall events
- Pre-monsoon testing and system preparation is essential for municipal systems
- Extreme rainfall above 100mm in 24 hours requires emergency protocol activation

## Temperature Effects on Water Quality
- Water temperature above 30 degrees Celsius accelerates bacterial growth by 50%
- High temperature reduces dissolved oxygen, stressing aquatic systems
- Temperature above 35 degrees indicates infrastructure heating problems
- Cold water (below 5 degrees) can cause pipe contraction and bursts
- Ideal water temperature for distribution: 10-20 degrees Celsius
- Monitor temperature with pH as combined indicators of treatment effectiveness

## Division-Specific Monitoring Standards
- Industrial zones: heavy metals, COD, BOD, petroleum hydrocarbons
- Agricultural zones: nitrates, phosphates, organochlorine pesticides, herbicides
- Residential zones: bacterial contamination, TDS, pH, residual chlorine
- Hospital/school areas: zero tolerance for any contamination parameter
- Sensitive zones must be tested every 4 hours minimum
- All zone alerts must reach Division Health Officer within 15 minutes

## Indian Regulatory Standards
- Bureau of Indian Standards IS 10500:2012 for drinking water quality
- Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) Schedule VI for surface water
- National Water Quality Sub-Mission monitoring protocols
- Jal Jeevan Mission FHTC testing guidelines (Functional Household Tap Connection)
- State Pollution Control Board quarterly inspection requirements
- National Green Tribunal directions on groundwater protection
- Ministry of Jal Shakti WQMS (Water Quality Monitoring System) reporting

## Predictive Risk Assessment
- Risk doubles when rainfall exceeds 40mm in 24 hours combined with turbidity above 5 NTU
- Temperature above 30 degrees combined with pH below 7 indicates bacterial growth risk
- TDS above 400 mg/L combined with pH below 6.5 indicates industrial leaching
- Turbidity spike of more than 5 NTU per hour indicates emergency contamination event
- Three consecutive moderate-risk readings from same sensor indicates infrastructure failure
- Upstream contamination reaches downstream stations within 2-4 hours typically
- Monsoon season risk: bacterial contamination probability increases by 3 times
- Post-flood: mandatory 48-hour testing protocol before restoring supply
